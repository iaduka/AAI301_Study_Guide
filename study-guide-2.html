Bias & Fairness Dilemma

A visual guide to understanding how systematic errors creep into AI, how we define fairness, and the complex challenge of holding machines (and humans) accountable.

Where Does Bias Come From?

Bias isn't just a "bug"; it's often a reflection of the world or the process. It systematically skews outputs, favoring some groups while disadvantaging others.

1. Data Bias

Flaws in the training material.
Ex: Historical bias showing mostly men as doctors.

2. Algorithmic Bias

Model design choices.
Ex: Optimization goals that prioritize accuracy over equity.

3. User Bias

How humans interact.
Ex: Users accepting biased outputs, creating a feedback loop.

Real-World Consequences

Case Study: Predictive Policing

An algorithm assigns patrols based on "Total Reported Incidents."

The Feedback Loop: Sending more police to "Neighborhood Blue" (due to 'discovered' crimes like drug stops) leads to more arrests, which justifies sending even more police, unfairly targeting the community.

Case Study: GenAI Stereotypes

Visualizing the output probability of demographics for specific job prompts (e.g., "CEO" vs "Nurse").

Cultural Bias: Western perspectives often dominate, stereotyping specific cultures (e.g., "money-minded" vs "laid back") based on scraped internet data.

The Accountability Web

Who is responsible when AI fails? The "Black Box" nature makes tracing errors difficult, leading to a Diffusion of Responsibility.

1
The Developer

Responsible for: Training data accuracy, model safety design.

2
The Deployer (University)

Responsible for: "Human in the Loop" oversight, appropriate use cases.

3
The User (Student)

Responsible for: Verifying critical info, ethical usage.
